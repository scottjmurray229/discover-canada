Tofino sits at the edge of Canada — perched on a narrow finger of Vancouver Island where the Pacific breaks hard against ancient forest and the weather comes in from thousands of kilometres of open ocean with nothing to slow it down. It is dramatic in the way that coastlines become when they have never been tamed, and it is one of the few places in Canada where you feel genuinely close to the wild.

## Surfing Long Beach

Long Beach stretches 16km between Tofino and Ucluelet, and it is the finest surf break in Canada. The swells generated by Pacific storms roll in with surprising consistency, and the water — cold, clear, and powerful — has built a genuine surf culture in this remote corner of the country. Surf schools operate year-round for beginners, and the more experienced will find plenty of challenge in the bigger sets.

You do not need to surf to enjoy Long Beach. The beach itself is extraordinary — enormous, wind-swept, and almost always dramatic. At low tide the sand stretches seemingly forever. In storm season, the waves tower and crash with a force that you feel as much as hear.

## Grey Whale Migration

From March through May, an estimated 20,000 grey whales migrate past the Tofino coast on their journey from Baja California to the Bering Sea. They travel close to shore — sometimes within metres of the beach — and the migration is one of Canada's great wildlife spectacles. Local whale-watching boats get you close to the action, but on good days you can see spouts from the beach without binoculars.

## Clayoquot Sound

The waters behind Tofino's ocean-facing coast are Clayoquot Sound — a vast, calm UNESCO Biosphere Reserve of islands, inlets, ancient rainforest, and wildlife. Kayaking here is transformative. Old-growth cedars line the water's edge, bald eagles circle overhead, sea otters float on their backs eating urchins, and the only sound is your paddle. Hot Springs Cove, accessible by boat or floatplane, offers natural thermal pools in a coastal forest setting.

## The Food Scene

For a town of 1,900 people, Tofino punches well above its weight in food quality. The Pointe Restaurant at the Wickaninnish Inn serves Pacific Northwest cuisine with an ocean view that makes every dish taste better. Rhino Coffee keeps the surf community fuelled. The Wolf in the Fog has won national awards and deserves every one. Freshly landed Dungeness crab, halibut fish and chips, and locally harvested sea asparagus and kelp make their way onto menus from June through September.

## Storm Watching

Tofino's storm-watching season (November through February) has become one of Canada's most distinctive travel experiences. Lodges like the Wickaninnish Inn have been built specifically for this purpose — floor-to-ceiling windows facing the Pacific, stone fireplaces, hot tubs, and the spectacle of winter fronts driving wave after wave against the coast. It sounds implausible that people travel specifically to watch bad weather, but the experience is genuinely compelling.
