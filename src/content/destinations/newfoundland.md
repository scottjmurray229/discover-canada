Newfoundland is where Canada gets strange, in the best possible way. The island occupies its own time zone (Newfoundland Standard Time — 30 minutes ahead of the Maritime provinces, a choice that perfectly captures the province's relationship with everyone else's conventions), speaks its own dialect, and maintains a culture so distinct from mainland Canada that visitors often remark it feels like a different country.

What it has that nowhere else does: icebergs drifting past coastal headlands in May and June, Viking ruins that predate Columbus by 500 years, puffin colonies that exist nowhere else on the continent at this density, humpback whales breaching in bays overlooked by wooden row houses, and a hospitality so genuine and enthusiastic it becomes almost overwhelming.

## St. John's

The capital of Newfoundland is one of North America's most distinctive cities — row houses painted in jewel tones climbing a hill that looks directly out to the North Atlantic, a harbor that has been used by European fishing boats since the 1500s, and a pub culture on George Street that runs deep into the early hours. The screech-in ceremony, where a cod is kissed and a shot of Newfoundland rum consumed in exchange for official honorary Newfoundlander status, is a tourist ritual but one that locals perform with genuine warmth.

Signal Hill National Historic Site, at the mouth of the harbor, is where Marconi received the first transatlantic wireless signal in 1901 and where the views over the city and ocean are the best in town.

## Iceberg Alley

Every spring, icebergs calved from Greenland's glaciers drift south on the Labrador Current and pass along Newfoundland's northeast coast in a phenomenon called Iceberg Alley. The icebergs can reach extraordinary sizes — some as tall as 15-story buildings — and they glow blue-white in the sunlight, drifting past fishing villages and coastal cliffs with a surreal, unhurried majesty.

Twillingate, on the northeast coast, is the best-known iceberg viewing location. The boat tours take you close enough to feel the cold radiating off the ice. May and June are peak viewing months.

## Gros Morne National Park

On the island's west coast, Gros Morne National Park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site containing some of the most remarkable geological scenery in the world. The Tablelands — a flat-topped massif of peridotite rock that was once the Earth's mantle, thrust to the surface by tectonic collision — look exactly like Mars, in a russet-red, alien landscape where virtually nothing grows. Western Brook Pond is a freshwater fjord accessible only by boat, with 600-metre cliffs rising sheer from still water. The combination of geology, scenery, and wilderness in Gros Morne is genuinely exceptional.

## L'Anse aux Meadows

At the very tip of Newfoundland's Northern Peninsula, L'Anse aux Meadows is the only confirmed Norse settlement in North America, built around 1000 CE — five centuries before Columbus. The reconstructed sod buildings sit above a peat bog in a landscape of wind-bent spruce and granite, and the interpretation centre tells the story of Leif Eriksson's expedition with archaeological precision. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most historically significant places in the Western Hemisphere.

## The Food

Newfoundland food is not for the faint-hearted, and it is not trying to impress anyone. Fish and brewis (salt cod with hard bread) has sustained fishing families for centuries. Cod tongues are sautéed in butter and served at local diners for lunch. Toutons — deep-fried bread dough — are served with partridgeberry jam for breakfast. Salt beef hash, figgy duff pudding, and partridgeberry everything round out a food culture that is entirely its own. The craft beer scene in St. John's has developed rapidly — Yellowbelly and Quidi Vidi brewing both make excellent beers.
